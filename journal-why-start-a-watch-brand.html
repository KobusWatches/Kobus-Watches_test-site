Why start a watch brand?

The first in a series of posts from behind the scenes at Kobus, on what it actually takes to turn a collector's obsession into a watch brand.

There is a question every founder gets asked, and it usually arrives with a raised eyebrow: why on earth would you start a watch brand? The market is crowded. Most new microbrands don't survive their first few years. The Swiss giants have marketing budgets that can wipe the floor with new entrants. And let's be honest: does the world really need another watch?

All true. And yet here I am. Let me try to answer the question from my point of view.

The itch to build something of my own

For more than ten years I have been a collector. I know the ritual well: researching obscure references and new releases at night — and, if I'm honest, during work. Many of us went down that rabbit hole: the strap changes nobody else notices, the small ceremony of winding a watch in the morning. Then I found something many collectors never will: I was content with my collection. There was no next watch I was hunting. And with that hunt gone, the energy behind it had nowhere to go — but it didn't disappear. It turned from buying watches to the industry itself: maybe I should work for a brand, maybe sell watches professionally, anything to stay close to the craft.

After a while I realised it was mostly my creative side talking, urging the rational part of my brain to create something.

A watch brand starts as a notebook, some sketches and a name nobody has heard of. Every decision after that — the length of a lug, the weight of a crown, the typeface on the dial — is yours to get right or wrong. In a professional life spent working within large, slow-moving institutions, that kind of total ownership is rare. That, more than anything, is the honest answer to "why."

So the seed was planted, but many questions were still unanswered. What type of watch would I create? Where would I draw inspiration from? Who would my clients be? My first instinct was to make a modern version of a vintage wristwatch. Many before me have done it with great success; the design language is already there.

I have lived in Brussels for six years. I walk through the city daily and love living here — it's vibrant, young, and never stops surprising you. During a cultural weekend with my family, we visited the Horta Museum, where Victor Horta lived and designed many of the Art Nouveau buildings Brussels is known for. Then and there I knew where to draw my inspiration from. It's a very specific direction, which makes the design even harder.

Naturally, I started researching watches from that era, and quickly ran into a problem: there were barely any commercial wristwatches at the dawn of the twentieth century. A vintage-inspired watch was no longer an option — but more on that in a later journal entry, where we explore the elements of this design.

Everything you suddenly need

Here is what they don't tell you: designing the watch is only a fraction of the work. The moment you decide to build a brand, a list unfolds that never seems to end.

You need a name that survives a trademark search and can be pronounced in most languages. A logo and wordmark that still look right at three millimetres tall on a dial. A website, product photography, legal terms, a company structure, contracts with designers and suppliers.

You need a design language coherent enough that multiple models can be created and recognised immediately. This is a real fork in the road. The fast route is off-the-shelf: manufacturers offer catalogues of existing cases, dials and hands, and you assemble a watch from parts a dozen other brands also use. It's cheaper, quicker and lower-risk — and it's why so many new releases look interchangeable. The slow route is building your own design language from scratch, where every component is drawn for your brand and nobody else's. It costs more in every way, but it's the only route where a second and third model strengthen the first instead of starting from zero. We took the slow route; the next journal entry explains how.

And before everything else, you need funding. I put it last on the list, but it's the item that decides whether the rest is real or a fantasy.

I was fortunate to work with people who have spent their careers in this industry, on design and on branding. That was a deliberate choice. A first watch gets exactly one first impression, and enthusiasts can smell a template product from across the room.

The price question

Before you draw a single case profile, you have to answer one uncomfortable question: where should your watch sit in price?

Everything follows from that number. It dictates your movement, your case manufacturing, your finishing, your margins, and who your customer is. Price it too low and you're competing with mass-produced pieces on volume you'll never match. Price it too high and you're asking people to pay heritage prices for a brand with no heritage yet.

For our first watch, we wanted a price high enough to allow some bold creative decisions in the design, but low enough to compete with the entry-level models of bigger brands. Indicatively that means between €1,250 and €1,500, though production costs could still shift this range. It's the territory where a genuinely Swiss-made, mechanical, thoughtfully designed watch can exist without cutting corners — and where we can explain what every euro pays for.

From passion to business

The romantic version of this story ends there. The real version continues into spreadsheets.

A watch is a brutally capital-intensive product. Suppliers ask for deposits long before you hold a finished watch, tooling for a custom case is a four-figure line item on its own, and minimum order quantities mean you commit to hundreds of pieces before selling one. All in, bringing a serious first watch to market is not a small thing. From that point on, it's cash-flow planning, supplier negotiations and a business plan solid enough to convince a bank.

That transition — from enthusiast to entrepreneur — is the hardest part of the whole journey. The watch you love as a collector and the watch that works as a business are not automatically the same watch. Learning to hold both perspectives at once is, I presume, part of what separates the brands that last from the ones that don't.

Why not Kickstarter

Which brings us to the question every new brand faces: how do you finance production?

A commonly used method is Kickstarter. It has launched hundreds of microbrands and proven that it works. We decided against it, deliberately. A crowdfunding page frames your watch as a project seeking validation, and that framing has a cost. Perceived value is fragile for a young brand: a watch first seen next to a funding thermometer and an early-bird discount is hard to reposition later as a piece worth its full price. And the platform itself has changed. By now, plenty of watch campaigns have never delivered, and backers have noticed. What once signalled scrappy independence now, fairly or not, carries a hint of risk.

It's a fine way to raise money and a difficult way to build a brand meant to be around in twenty years. The brand has to outlive the campaign.

Instead, Kobus will launch with a pre-sale: a limited number of pieces offered first to the people who have followed the brand from the beginning, at preferential terms, before general availability. It serves the same honest purpose — proving demand before committing fully to production — but on our own terms, on our own site, with a direct relationship to every early owner.

So, why start a watch brand?

Because the alternative was spending another ten years admiring other people's work. And because Brussels is full of Art Nouveau design, and almost nobody has seriously tried to put it on a watch.

The first Kobus is coming. In the next posts, I'll take you deeper into the design, the production, and everything we learn along the way.

— Mathieu, founder of Kobus
